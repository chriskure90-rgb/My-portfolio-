Developing Student Feedback Literacy with AI & Learning Analytics

An adaptive learning system that transforms how students engage with instructor feedback — combining an AI reflection tool with a learning analytics dashboard to close the gap between receiving feedback and acting on it.

Framework Nicol & Macfarlane-Dick (2006)
Role Instructional Designer
Topic Feedback Literacy / SRL

The Problem

Instructor feedback is one of the most powerful tools in education — but most students never use it properly. Instead of treating feedback as a roadmap for growth, students check their score and move on. The feedback loop is broken before it begins.

The Gap Between Ideal and Reality

The Ideal: Students become "Active Feedback Reviewers" — they decode feedback, plan strategies, and improve their skills (Nicol & Macfarlane-Dick, 2006).

The Reality: Feedback is used to justify grades, not for growth. Students ignore it after checking their score. Skill development stalls — there is no "feed-forward."

Theoretical Foundation

This project is grounded in Nicol & Macfarlane-Dick's (2006) seven principles of good feedback practice, which frames effective feedback as a self-regulatory process. Rather than treating feedback as teacher-to-student transmission, their model centers the student as an active agent who must interpret, internalize, and act on feedback.

Three principles directly informed the design: teachers need to clarify goals and communicate standards; students need structured opportunities to reflect on their learning; and both need data-driven support to close the gap between current performance and desired outcomes.

The Solution: Two Interlocking Components

Rather than a single tool, this project proposes a two-component system that addresses both sides of the feedback problem:

1

AI Reflection Tool (For Students)

An AI-powered reflection space that provides structured self-explanation prompts and active dialogue. Students move from passive "grade checkers" to active planners — guided to interpret their feedback, connect it to their goals, and identify specific improvement strategies. The AI never gives answers; it guides reasoning.

2

Integrated Learning Analytics Dashboard (For Teachers)

A data dashboard that synthesizes student engagement logs and AI rubric scores into actionable insights. Teachers can instantly spot growth or stagnation, identify specific reflection weaknesses, and prioritize at-risk students who need immediate support — without manually reviewing every submission.

Data Architecture

The system collects two distinct types of data to build a complete picture of student engagement:

📋 Engagement Data (Behavioral)

  • Feedback Checked: Binary (Yes/No)
  • Time to Access: Delay after publication
  • AI Conversation Turns: Depth of interaction
  • Material Access: Other class resources
  • Link Access: External support clicks

🧠 Cognitive Data (AI Rubric Scores)

  • Interpreting Score: Quality of sense-making (1–4)
  • Action Plan Score: Specificity of goal-setting (1–4)
  • Mentioning Score: Resource utilization awareness (1–4)

These two data streams are combined into a unified event log — merging LMS logs and AI interactions, applying consistent naming schemas, and assigning session IDs to map continuous learning flows.

Adaptive Logic: Three Learner Profiles

By crossing behavioral and cognitive data, the system identifies three distinct learner types and triggers targeted interventions for each:

Type 1

Resourceful Learner

High engagement, high metacognition. Clicks resources, reflects deeply.

→ Acknowledge progress and offer advanced resources.
Type 2

"Surface" Checker

Low engagement, low metacognition. Quick exit, surface-level text.

→ Trigger automated nudge to revisit feedback.
Type 3

Trial & Error Learner

Medium engagement, low-medium metacognition. Repeated chats, no resources.

→ Provide direct scaffolding to specific class resources.

Dashboard Capabilities

The analytics dashboard translates raw data into two types of actionable value:

🎯 For Teaching Decisions

  • Progress tracking across assignments
  • AI-pinpointed reflection weaknesses
  • Data-driven talking points for 1-on-1s
  • Intervention alerts for at-risk students

🔍 For Skill Analysis

  • Behavioral profiling of help-seeking skills
  • Root cause analysis beyond low grades
  • Tailored counseling dialogue prompts
  • Automated workshop recommendations

Why This Matters

Most feedback interventions focus on improving the quality of feedback itself. This project takes a different approach: the feedback is already there — the problem is that students don't know how to use it. By combining structured AI reflection with behavioral analytics, this system targets the moment where feedback most often fails: the transition from receiving to acting.

The result is a system where students develop genuine feedback literacy — not just by being told to "use feedback," but by being scaffolded through the cognitive process of interpretation, planning, and action in a structured, low-stakes environment.

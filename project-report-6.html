Usability & UX Research: Kreebo Reading App

A full-cycle UX investigation that combined ecosystem mapping, usability testing, and WCAG auditing to reveal how one hidden accessibility failure was silently breaking the entire user experience.

Duration 3 months
Role UX Researcher
Topic EdTech / UX Research

The Challenge

Kreebo is an AI-powered app designed to encourage children to read and create their own stories. On the surface, it has strong educational potential. But potential alone doesn't make a product usable — and nobody had systematically asked where the experience was breaking down, or why.

My goal was to answer exactly that: run a structured, multi-method UX investigation and turn the findings into evidence that could drive real product decisions.

My Approach

Rather than jumping straight into user testing, I started by understanding the ecosystem. I mapped eight stakeholder groups — Teachers, Students, Parents, School Administrators, Curriculum Developers, Librarians, Product Developers, and a Data Security body — and traced how their needs and constraints interact.

Key Insight from Ecosystem Mapping

Two structural tensions surfaced immediately: Teachers needed personalized assignments, but the app's monolingual design excluded bilingual families. And School Administrators needed content consistency, but the AI's generative nature made that impossible to guarantee. These weren't user complaints — they were design-level contradictions embedded in the product architecture.

From there, I built a learner journey map to pinpoint exactly where in the experience these tensions would surface — then ran usability testing with four parents to confirm it with real behavior.

What I Found

Using Nielsen's Heuristics as my diagnostic framework, I identified five friction points. But the most important insight wasn't about any single finding — it was about the pattern connecting them.

1

The System Was Silent When It Broke

When the microphone failed during story creation, the app showed nothing — no error, no guidance. All 4 participants were stranded. This wasn't just a UX annoyance; it was a failure of Heuristic 1 (Visibility of System Status) that made the product's most creative feature completely inaccessible.

2

The AI Was Doing Too Much, Too Fast

The story generation phase asked users an excessive number of questions in sequence. 3 of 4 participants disengaged before finishing. The AI's flexibility became a burden — violating Heuristic 7 (Flexibility and Efficiency of Use) by demanding too much from users with no shortcut.

3

A Mandatory Task Was Working Against the Product's Own Goals

After creating a story, users were required to write an English summary — a task participants described as too challenging for children. This generated the highest proportion of negative sentiment in the entire study, turning a moment of accomplishment into a frustrating obligation.

4

The Reading Experience Was a Clear Success

The core reading phase produced 27 positive codes and zero negative codes — the strongest performance of any task phase. This contrast made the surrounding friction points even more significant: the product works when it lets users read, and struggles when it asks them to produce.

The Insight That Tied It Together

The WCAG 2.2 accessibility audit revealed a critical failure in WCAG 3.3.1 (Error Identification): the app relied entirely on visual cues with no text-based error descriptions or ARIA labels.

Triangulation Moment

This single accessibility failure directly explained the microphone crisis. The system wasn't just bad UX — it was technically incapable of communicating errors to users. The qualitative behavior (100% of participants stranded), the quantitative data (10 "Confused" codes during story generation), and the accessibility audit all pointed to the same root cause. That's the value of triangulation: it turns observations into evidence.

27 Positive codes in Reading phase
10 Confused codes in Story Generation
12 Negative codes in Summary Task
4/4 Participants hit by silent mic failure

What I Recommended — and Why It Matters

Evidence without direction isn't useful. I translated the findings into three prioritized recommendations:

  • Fix error communication first. Adding text-based error messages and ARIA labels resolves both the accessibility failure and the microphone crisis in one move — the highest-leverage fix available.
  • Reduce AI friction with defaults. Introducing "Quick Generate" templates lets users bypass the exhausting prompt sequence without removing flexibility for those who want it.
  • Rethink the Summary Task. Replace the mandatory high-stakes writing task with a lower-friction comprehension check — like a simple multiple choice question — that preserves the learning goal without punishing users for completing a story.
